Project 1: Identifying High-Risk Texas Counties for Hurricane Impact Using Latitude and Population Data

Danielle Balque

View on GitHub

This project aimed to identify Texas counties most vulnerable to hurricanes based on their geographical location and population size.

Data Cleaning and Exploration:

Our team began by importing and cleaning GIS data on Texas counties, removing duplicates and missing values. We then analyzed the latitude and longitude coordinates to assess each county's geographical positioning.

Hurricane-Prone Zones:

We found that hurricane-prone zones lie between latitudes 5-30. We visualized this through scatter plots, highlighting counties in this range. Notably, these counties are primarily in southern Texas.

Joint Plots and Clustering:

To delve deeper into the spatial distribution of Texas counties, we created joint plots to visualize relationships between latitude and longitude coordinates. These plots offer insights into how counties are geographically clustered.

Northeast Clustering:

The visualizations show that counties are densely clustered in the northeastern regions, which are generally outside of the hurricane-prone zones.

Coastal Distribution:

In contrast, counties along coastal regions exhibit a more dispersed distribution, aligning with the hurricane-prone zone findings.

Density Representations:

The joint plots use color gradations to indicate clustering density. Darker regions show areas where data points are more densely packed, while lighter regions reflect sparser distributions.

Population Impact:

We also analyzed population data to assess which counties had the highest populations and intersected with hurricane-prone zones. The top ten counties by population were visualized, with four of these counties also being in the hurricane-prone zone: Bexar, Fort Bend, Harris, and Hidalgo. The final plot emphasized these high-risk counties with enlarged markers.

Conclusion:

The analysis demonstrated that Texas has a considerable number of counties at risk from hurricanes, particularly in the southern regions. Furthermore, several of these counties also have large populations, indicating a significant potential impact from hurricane events.

Project 2: FBI, Open Up! Predicting Culprits From Crime TV Show Scripts

View on GitHub

Introduction

This project explores the relationship between linguistic patterns and the offender status of characters in crime TV shows, applying a psychological perspective to analyze their language and deception. The research collected dialogues from 10 different TV shows, analyzing them with the Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) software. This study aimed to uncover correlations between language features and offender status to contribute to a nuanced understanding of crime TV dramas and their portrayal of culprits.

Data Collection and Analysis

The project selected 10 TV shows from IMDb’s top 50 list, focusing on crime-solving dramas. Dialogues from these shows were manually extracted and analyzed using the LIWC tool to identify linguistic characteristics of culprits and innocent characters. This data was then fed into a linear regression model, achieving an R-squared value of 1.0, while a logistic regression model reached an accuracy rate of 54.5%.

Findings

The correlation analysis showed that characters exhibiting angry or sad emotions were more likely to be labeled as culprits, while those demonstrating logical or positive tones were less likely to be culprits. This highlights significant linguistic differences between culprits and innocent characters, contributing to understanding the portrayal of crime in media.

Conclusion

While the project’s findings demonstrate valuable insights into linguistic patterns associated with offenders in crime TV shows, it also acknowledges limitations, including the inconsistency of word count methods and their inability to capture contextual nuances. Further research could refine these models, expanding understanding of linguistic and psychological dimensions in crime TV dramas and their impact on media representation.
